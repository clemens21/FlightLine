# Gameplay Loop And Progression

## Core Session Loop

A typical play session should cycle through four activities:

1. Review company status.
2. Evaluate available work and constraints.
3. Commit aircraft to schedules.
4. Advance time and assess outcomes.

This loop needs to stay satisfying whether the player has one aircraft or fifty.

## Minute-To-Minute Gameplay

The most common player actions in MVP should be:

- checking aircraft availability and condition
- comparing contracts by margin, fit, and timing
- assigning flights or multi-leg work
- choosing whether to reposition, wait, or maintain
- advancing time and reacting to results

The player should rarely be forced into low-value administrative steps.

## Company Progression Arc

### Phase 1: Founder Operator

Fantasy:

- One aircraft or a tiny fleet.
- Tight cashflow.
- Every assignment matters.

Primary decisions:

- lease versus buy
- safe contracts versus aggressive reach
- keeping the aircraft flying versus preserving condition

Player emotion:

- vulnerable but exciting

### Phase 2: Stable Small Carrier

Fantasy:

- Two to six aircraft.
- The company has identity.
- Missed utilization is now a visible cost.

Primary decisions:

- specialization by aircraft role
- balancing reliability and throughput
- selecting which regions or airport clusters to serve

Player emotion:

- competence and momentum

### Phase 3: Regional Network Builder

Fantasy:

- A recognizable operation with multiple aircraft classes.
- Scheduling and fleet composition become strategic.

Primary decisions:

- standardization versus flexibility
- opening new bases or focus regions
- using automation for repeatable work
- choosing high-margin niches versus broad coverage

Player emotion:

- ownership and control

### Phase 4: Mature Operator

Fantasy:

- The company runs a meaningful network.
- The player is steering policy more than individual flights.

Primary decisions:

- portfolio management across aircraft types and business lines
- capital allocation
- reputation protection
- system-wide resilience during disruptions

Player emotion:

- mastery

## Progression Systems

MVP progression should come from four axes:

- cash and access to financing
- reputation and contract quality
- fleet capability
- operational capacity through tools and automation

This matters because a bigger bank account alone is not enough. Growth should change what opportunities are available and how much complexity the player can handle.

## Unlock Philosophy

Use soft unlocks where possible.

Examples:

- better reputation increases contract quality and urgency premiums
- stronger finances unlock better lease terms
- larger operations justify automation tools
- experience with a region reveals clearer demand patterns

Avoid arbitrary video-game gating unless it protects pacing or tutorialization.

## Failure And Recovery

The game needs setbacks, but not spiral-of-death design.

Potential failures:

- low cash
- contract penalties
- aircraft grounded for maintenance
- poor reputation reducing contract access

Recovery tools:

- lower-risk local contracts
- emergency leasing or short-term financing
- selling underperforming aircraft
- temporary downsizing of the schedule

The player should feel consequences without feeling trapped.

## Time Advancement

Time advancement is one of the core verbs of the game.

Requirements:

- advancing time should always feel intentional
- the player should understand what will happen during the selected time jump
- key interruptions should surface if aircraft finish, miss deadlines, or require maintenance

Suggested cadence for MVP:

- short jump for tactical dispatching
- medium jump for same-day operations
- long jump for overnight or background company progress

## Short-Term And Long-Term Goals

Short-term goals:

- keep aircraft profitable
- complete contracts on time
- avoid preventable downtime

Mid-term goals:

- add aircraft
- specialize the fleet
- improve schedule efficiency
- grow reputation

Long-term goals:

- dominate a region or niche
- run a resilient multi-aircraft operation
- maintain high reliability with strong margins
- build a company identity around strategy, not brute scale alone

## What Makes Progression Feel Good

Progression should create visible capability gains:

- more contracts become viable
- scheduling becomes easier, not harder
- the player can survive disruption more comfortably
- the company can pursue deliberate strategy instead of taking whatever work appears

## MVP Progression Milestones

A first-pass milestone ladder could be:

- first profitable week
- first second aircraft
- first maintenance-induced operational problem solved correctly
- first high-value contract chain completed on time
- first region expansion
- first profitable multi-aircraft week with no missed deadlines

These milestones can later inform tutorials, achievements, and onboarding.
