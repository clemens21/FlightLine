# Product Pillars

## Vision

FlightLine is a modern airline operations and fleet management sim where the player grows from a small operator into a sophisticated air transport company by making better operational decisions, not by surviving opaque spreadsheets or repetitive clicking.

## Target Player

Primary player:

- Likes management games with persistent progression.
- Enjoys aviation enough to care about aircraft capabilities and airport constraints.
- Wants authenticity, but not a full dispatch or maintenance certification course.
- Values optimization, planning, and building a company over direct piloting.
- Wants to expand on microsoft flight sim management capability and run a company while also being an employee of that company and flying their own missions

Secondary player:

- Already plays aviation sims and wants a meaningful management layer around them.
- Likes the fantasy of owning and scaling an airline, charter, or cargo business.
- Wants automation support once the company grows.

Not the target for MVP:

- Players seeking a pure tycoon game with no operational realism.
- Players expecting full flight sim fidelity from day one.
- Players who want heavy crew roleplay or hardcore accounting as the main experience.

## Player Promise

The game should consistently deliver this feeling:

- I can understand my company.
- I can see why a decision is good or bad.
- My aircraft choices matter.
- My company gets more capable as it grows.
- I am running operations, not doing clerical cleanup.

## Pillar 1: Clarity Over Opacity

Every major outcome should be explainable.

This means:

- route profitability previews before dispatch
- visible reasons for failed validation
- transparent maintenance condition and risk
- reports that connect company performance to actual operating choices

If the player loses money, misses deadlines, or suffers poor reliability, the game should show why.

## Pillar 2: Strategy Over Chores

The game should ask the player to make decisions that matter and reduce repetitive steps that do not.

This means:

- batch scheduling tools
- reusable operating rules
- automation for routine dispatch later
- defaults that prevent unnecessary data entry

Manual interaction should feel like control, not paperwork.

## Pillar 3: Authenticity Where It Creates Tradeoffs

Real aviation details are valuable when they create interesting constraints.

Keep:

- runway limits
- range and payload tradeoffs
- maintenance downtime
- aircraft utilization pressure
- airport suitability

Avoid early overreach into details that do not improve strategy:

- exhaustive crew rosters
- minute operational paperwork
- deep cabin service simulation
- granular technical systems with no gameplay payoff

## Pillar 4: Growth Should Change How You Play

Scaling from one aircraft to twenty should not just multiply the same clicks.

Early game should emphasize:

- survival
- careful aircraft acquisition
- route selection
- cash discipline

Mid game should emphasize:

- utilization
- reliability
- scheduling efficiency
- specialization choices

Late game should emphasize:

- network shape
- fleet standardization versus flexibility
- automation rules
- capital allocation

## Pillar 5: Data-Dense, Modern, and Calm

The interface should feel contemporary and operational, not like a legacy enterprise form.

This means:

- strong hierarchy for key numbers
- dense information without visual clutter
- fast comparisons between options
- modern light and dark themes with equal care
- tables, maps, cards, and timelines used intentionally

The UI should reward frequent use.

## Product Differentiators Versus the Baseline Inspiration

FlightLine should improve on the baseline genre by focusing on:

- clearer profitability and fit analysis for contracts and routes
- less friction in scheduling and rescheduling
- more legible maintenance and downtime planning
- stronger company progression beyond accumulating cash
- better support for scaling operations without turning the game into admin work

## Anti-Pillars

The game should avoid becoming:

- a spreadsheet with aircraft art
- a shallow tycoon with aviation skin
- a direct clone of OnAir
- a flight simulator dependency disguised as a management game
- a micromanagement treadmill that becomes worse as the fleet grows

## Success Test

A strong FlightLine session should let a player answer yes to these questions:

- Do I know what my best aircraft should be doing next?
- Do I understand which contracts are worth taking?
- Can I tell whether my company is becoming healthier or riskier?
- Does growth unlock better decisions, not just more work?
